4.1 The Revenue Concentration Problem

Battery revenue in the NEM is not evenly distributed across time. A small fraction of hours drive the majority of returns, making location optimization critical for economic viability.

Battery economics depend entirely on capturing extreme price events. Missing even a few of these critical hours‚Äîdue to curtailment, poor MLF, or grid constraints‚Äîmaterially impacts annual returns.

Critical implications:

  1. Extreme concentration: 50% of annual revenue is generated in just ~10% of operating hours
  2. Volatility dependence: Battery economics depend on price extremes, not average prices
  3. Location sensitivity: Missing key high-price events (due to curtailment, poor MLF, or grid constraints) materially impacts returns

‚ö†Ô∏è Why This Matters for Co-location

  • DC-coupled batteries may miss high-price discharge events if solar is generating (constrained by inverter capacity)
  • Poor MLF locations compound revenue loss during critical high-price periods
  • Transmission curtailment disproportionately impacts returns by eliminating top revenue hours

The investment decision hinges on capturing these fat-tail events. Standalone batteries optimize for this by siting in high-MLF locations with minimal curtailment risk.

4.2 Marginal Loss Factors: The Hidden Tax

4.2.1 Understanding MLF Impact

Marginal Loss Factors (MLFs) represent the energy lost when electricity passes through the transmission network. Generators are paid (or pay) based on MLF-adjusted prices:

MLF Value Meaning Typical Location
> 1.0 Premium received Near load centers (Sydney, Newcastle)
= 1.0 Neutral (hub reference) Regional reference nodes
< 1.0 Discount applied End-of-line, high transmission losses

Example calculation: If wholesale price = $100/MWh and MLF = 0.90:

  • Generator receives: $100 √ó 0.90 = $90/MWh
  • 10% discount due to transmission losses

‚ÑπÔ∏è MLF Impact on Battery Arbitrage

For batteries arbitraging price spreads, MLF affects both discharge revenue and charging cost:

  • Discharge: Revenue discounted by MLF
  • Charge: Cost discounted by MLF (pay less to charge)

Net impact depends on price spread and MLF differential between charge/discharge locations.

4.4 Network Constraints and Curtailment Risk

4.4.1 The Transmission Bottleneck

Even with favorable MLF, batteries face curtailment risk when transmission networks are constrained. This disproportionately impacts revenue by eliminating top-earning hours.

Curtailment mechanisms:

  1. System security constraints: Generator output restricted to maintain frequency/stability
  2. Network limits: Transmission lines at capacity, exports restricted
  3. Local network issues: Voltage or thermal limits in distribution networks

üö® Impact on Revenue Concentration

Since 50% of revenue comes from ~10% of hours (typically high-price periods with tight system conditions), curtailment during these events has an outsized impact on annual returns.

Co-location vulnerability:

  • Solar-heavy regions face more curtailment (midday surplus)
  • Co-located batteries may be curtailed when solar is exporting (network constraint)
  • Behind-the-meter batteries forced to charge/discharge to match solar, missing optimal arbitrage windows

Standalone advantage:

  • Independent dispatch can respond to market signals without solar constraints
  • High-MLF locations typically have better transmission access
  • FCAS services provide alternative revenue during curtailment events

4.5 The Location Optimization Framework

4.5.1 Site Selection Hierarchy

For battery investors, location optimization follows a clear hierarchy:

  1. MLF maximization (primary revenue driver)
  2. Curtailment risk minimization (protect top-decile revenue hours)
  3. Price volatility exposure (ensure sufficient price spread)
  4. FCAS market access (ancillary services revenue)
  5. Transmission capacity (ability to export full capacity)

4.5.2 When Co-location Makes Sense

‚ö°

Co-location Can Be Optimal When:

Conditions favoring hybrid deployment

  • Grid upgrades planned: Transmission improvements will improve MLF/reduce curtailment
  • Green premium sufficient: ESG buyers pay enough to offset MLF penalty
  • Retrofit scenario: Adding battery to existing solar (location constraint already exists)
  • CIS eligibility: Government contracts compensate for location disadvantage

4.5.3 When Standalone Is Preferred

üîã

Standalone Is Preferred When:

Conditions favoring independent deployment

  • Greenfield development: Free to choose optimal location
  • Merchant exposure: Targeting pure arbitrage without PPA protection
  • High hurdle rates: Need maximum revenue per MW
  • FCAS focus: Ancillary services revenue is significant

4.6 Beyond Revenue: Operational Considerations

4.6.1 Battery Duration and MLF Sensitivity

Longer-duration batteries (4hr+) are less sensitive to MLF than 2hr systems because:

  • Fewer cycles: Lower annual revenue per MW capacity, so MLF impact is diluted
  • Energy arbitrage focus: Capturing day-night spread rather than intraday volatility
  • Capacity payments: May be contracted for capacity rather than energy arbitrage

However, 2hr batteries (the current market standard) are highly MLF-sensitive because they rely on rapid cycling during price spikes ‚Äî the exact events where MLF adjusts the absolute revenue.

4.6.2 Degradation and Revenue Quality

High-MLF locations often correlate with:

  • More cycling: Volatile prices = frequent charge/discharge = faster degradation
  • Higher temperatures: Load centers = hotter climate = reduced battery life

Investors must balance:

  • Higher revenue (good MLF) vs Higher opex (increased maintenance, shorter replacement cycle)

Standalone batteries in high-MLF locations may face 15-20% higher degradation rates than co-located batteries in milder climates ‚Äî partially offsetting the revenue advantage.

Key Takeaways
  • Battery revenue is highly concentrated: 50% of revenue from ~10% of hours
  • MLF materially impacts returns: 15-25% revenue difference between poor (0.85) and excellent (0.98) locations
  • Solar farms typically have poor MLFs (0.80-0.90) due to end-of-line siting
  • Standalone batteries can access high-MLF locations (0.95-1.05) near load centers
  • Curtailment risk disproportionately impacts returns by eliminating top revenue hours
  • Location-driven revenue differential often exceeds capex savings from co-location
  • Duration matters: 2hr batteries more MLF-sensitive than 4hr+ systems
  • Operational factors (degradation, temperature) partially offset high-MLF advantages
